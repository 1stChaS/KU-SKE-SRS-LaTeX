In order to understand the current research landscape and identify systems with related capabilities, a competitor analysis is conducted based on recent studies in UAV simulation, gamified drone training, and AI-driven game content generation. Since no single work integrates all elements of the proposed system, the comparison focuses on closely related approaches that address important subproblems such as realistic drone simulation, gamified pilot training, and LLM-based content generation.

Čapek et al. [1] introduced FlightForge, an open-source UAV simulator built on Unreal Engine 5 that supports procedural terrain generation using Perlin noise, high-fidelity rendering, and multi-UAV deployment with RGB, RGB-D, and LiDAR sensors. While FlightForge provides a realistic simulation environment suitable for autonomous UAV research, it does not include gamification, real-world GIS terrain, mission planning tools, or any form of adaptive training for human pilots.

Cardona Reyes et al. [2] presented a VR-based gamified training system for university-level drone pilots. Their work demonstrates that gamification elements such as challenges, rewards, and progress tracking can improve engagement and learning outcomes for beginner pilots. However, the system uses a generic virtual environment without real-world terrain data and does not integrate real drone control protocols such as MAVLink or AI-based adaptive difficulty adjustment.

Gallotta et al. [3] provided the first comprehensive survey of LLM applications in games, identifying multiple roles LLMs can play including content generation, NPC dialogue, game mastering, and procedural content generation. The survey highlights that LLMs are particularly promising for generating narrative content and mission descriptions. However, the survey covers general game applications and does not address UAV training, drone simulation, or adaptive learning systems.

Ponomarenko and Kharchenko [4] propose using ArduPilot SITL with MAVLink to create realistic UAV simulations within gamified training environments. Their approach enables accurate drone behavior, real-time control, and telemetry collection, allowing users to practice safely while supporting performance evaluation and adaptive training.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{section:literature-review}

UAV simulators have become essential tools for both autonomous systems research and pilot training. A comprehensive survey on reinforcement learning-based drone simulators [5], published in Artificial Intelligence Review, reviews simulators built on Unity 3D and ROS, comparing their physics engines, sensor support, and RL integration. The survey highlights that most existing simulators focus on autonomous flight control rather than human pilot training, and none of them combine gamification with adaptive AI-driven difficulty adjustment. Similarly, TerrAInav Sim [6] is an open-source simulation tool that generates 3D environments from map-based data for UAV aerial imaging research. While it demonstrates the feasibility of using real-world map data for terrain generation, it is designed for imaging research and does not support mission planning training, gamification, or adaptive learning. These works establish that realistic UAV simulation is a well-explored area, but the application of such simulators for human-centered training with real-world terrain remains limited.

In the area of gamified drone training, research has consistently shown that gamification improves learner engagement and performance. Trujillo-Espinoza et al. [7] extended the gamification approach to younger learners by designing VR environments with simplified flight tasks and gamified feedback, confirming that gamification is effective for drone training across different age groups. A study published in PMC [8] evaluated a gamified drone training simulator (GDTS) by analyzing brain signals and keyboard logs to measure participant success, providing evidence that gamified simulators can be used to assess pilot performance through behavioral metrics. However, both works rely on generic virtual environments and simple input-based evaluation. Neither incorporates real drone control protocols, real-world terrain data, or AI-driven adaptive difficulty — all of which are critical for bridging the gap between game-based training and real-world drone operation.

Recent advances in Large Language Models have opened new possibilities for game content generation. A survey on procedural content generation with emerging LLM integration [9] reviews five years of PCG research and identifies LLMs as a promising new category of PCG algorithms capable of generating customizable game content through natural language prompts. Hu, Zhao, and Liu [10] demonstrated at the IEEE Conference on Games that LLMs can generate both game rules and levels simultaneously using video game description language, showing that LLMs can produce structured game content beyond simple text. Júnior, Adaniya, and Nunes [11] fine-tuned a DeepSeek-R1 model to generate customizable 2D game levels for a Zelda-like game, evaluating generated levels on playability, novelty, diversity, and accuracy with strong results. This demonstrates that fine-tuned LLMs can generate game content that meets specific constraints — directly relevant to generating missions that match specific difficulty levels and terrain types. Furthermore, Özkaya, Berrezueta-Guzman, and Wagner [12] surveyed 62 peer-reviewed studies on LLMs in VR, identifying that LLMs can enhance virtual reality experiences through dynamic content generation and personalization. These studies collectively support the feasibility of using LLMs for mission content generation in a training-oriented game environment.

From a UAV system perspective, flight telemetry data plays a crucial role in evaluating pilot performance. Features such as trajectory deviation, altitude stability, speed consistency, and control smoothness can be extracted from raw telemetry data transmitted through the MAVLink protocol. These features provide meaningful representations of user behavior and can serve as input for machine learning models. Contextual Bandit algorithms are particularly suitable for this purpose, as they can dynamically select the appropriate difficulty level based on the user's current performance context while balancing exploration and exploitation. Combined with Reinforcement Learning for optimizing the overall training path, this multi-model AI approach enables the system to continuously adapt to each user's skill progression.

In conclusion, while existing UAV simulators provide strong capabilities in flight simulation and terrain rendering, and gamified training systems have proven effective for learner engagement, there remains a significant gap in combining these elements with real-world terrain, real drone protocols, and adaptive AI. No existing system integrates real GIS terrain, MAVLink-based drone simulation, gamified training, and a multi-model AI architecture (LLM + Contextual Bandit + RL) into a single adaptive training platform. SantaTrail is designed to fill this gap by providing a closed-loop system where AI-generated missions adapt to the user's performance, creating a personalized and progressively challenging training experience for beginner drone pilots.
